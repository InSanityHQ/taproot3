Minimizing the Distance to a Subspace

Axler6.56 Minimizing the distance to a subspace

Suppose $U$ is a finite-dimensional subspace of $V$, $v \in V$, and $u \in U$. Then, \[\begin{aligned} \lVert v - P_U v \rVert \leq \lVert v - u \rVert \end{aligned}\]

Because we often end up having to find the minimal $v - u$ where $u \in U$, this result makes linear algebra applicable to numerous real-world applications.

Proof

\[\begin{aligned} \lVert v - P_U v \rVert ^2 &\leq \lVert v - P_U v \rVert ^2 + \lVert P_U v - u \rVert ^2 &\quad& \text{ by } 0 \leq \lVert P_U v - u \rVert ^2\\ &= \lVert (v - P_U v) + (P_U v - u) \rVert ^2 &\quad& \text{ by the Pythagorean Theorem }\\ &= \lVert v - u \rVert ^2 \end{aligned}\]

Inequality is an equality only when $u = P_U v$.

An example

First define an inner product that will be our cost function. In this case, they use the integral of $f(x) g(x)$ on the range $[ - \pi , \pi ]$. Then, orthonormalize a basis of the polynomials up to degree 6 (using the Gram-Schmidt procedure) and take the orthonormal projection using the same inner product. This ends up with roughly \[\begin{aligned} u(x) = 0.987862x - 0.155271x^3 + 0.00564312x^5 \end{aligned}\] Which ends up being a better approximation for the range than the corresponding 5-th degree Taylor polynomial.
